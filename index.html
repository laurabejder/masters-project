Crisis in the Currents: Alabama's Tug-of-War for the Future of Freshwater Biodiversity

By Laura Bejder August 11, 2023

The detector chirped and Paul Johnson and Brittany Barker-Jones went, head first, into Alabama’s Little Lower Cahaba River.

Snorkels rising above them, the researchers submerged for 10 minutes until they found a tiny creature with a big story to tell – a story about the conflict between humans and nature and how that plays out.

“I did it!” Barker-Jones says excitedly, presenting a greenish brown, grape sized mussel.

“Look at the growth,” Johnson says, examining the animal.

It is the Coosa Moccasinshell, one of a growing number of endangered species of freshwater mussels. This one can only be found in an eight-mile stretch of one river in Alabama. Today the researchers are searching for the 30 mussels they had grown in a laboratory, tagged with PIT tag trackers and released into the water last fall.

The Coosa Moccasinshell used to be found across most river basins in Alabama. But because of human modifications of their habitats, sedimentation and worsened water quality, the mussel has almost completely disappeared from the state’s rivers.

And this little mussel’s tale is told over and over across the Southeastern United States.. For decades, the region has been the scene of a silent biodiversity crisis hastened by the competition between humanity’s attempts to control nature and nature’s attempts to fight back.

Paul Johnson and Brittany Baker-Jones are searching the river bed for a Coosa Moccasinshell in the Little Lower Cahaba River, Ala., July 12, 2023. (Photo/Laura Bejder Jensen)

Covered by networks of extensive river basins – including the Mississippi, Cumberland, Mobile and Tennessee – the region has one of the richest freshwater ecosystems on the planet. More than one-third of North America’s known freshwater species are native to the rivers and waterways of Alabama alone.

But a growing number of them are on the brink of extinction or have already disappeared, raising concerns about the health of freshwater and the animals and people that depend on it.

This is widening concerns among scientists trying to figure out the long-term impact of the changes. The species that were lost and the ones that might follow are signs of the pressure put on one of the world's richest freshwater systems.

“It is not an over-exaggeration. This is the worst conservation crisis in the United States,” Johnson, whose decades of ecosystem research comes out in a rapid-fire stream of enthusiasm, said in an interview. “There aren’t any that are anywhere near the proportion that this is.”

A Columbia University analysis of data maintained by Natureserve, a storehouse of biodiversity information, shows that a combined 20 percent of the Southeast region’s freshwater species are at high or very high risk of extinction, at least in part, because human interference in the form of extensive water-damming, other development and climate change.

They face particularly high risk because they can survive in only selected parts of different ecosystems or that their populations have already declined significantly. For many of them, one catastrophic event – a flood or an oil spill in a river – could wipe out the entire species.

If a species is known to occur in a state, it is given a state rank from S1 to S5 by the state institution responsible for tracking species. The ranks span from critically imperiled to secure. The state ranks give a more precise picture than broader global ranks. For the overall analysis, each species was grouped by their highest state rank to avoid categorizing a species as threatened if it is thriving in another state.

The analysis found that levels across the Southeast range from 36 percent imperiled and critically imperiled in Alabama to 19 percent in Louisiana.

Alabama also has the highest number of aquatic species that have gone extinct. Exactly 100 species – 12 percent of the species in the state – are no longer there.

The increased peril facing these animals is not just a problem for each individual species of fish, mussels, snails and crayfish. It is connected to larger issues with water quality in the region, the species' significance for the larger ecosystems and more recently, climate change mitigation.

Turning around or even slowing down the development takes more than conservation biology. It requires partnerships with industries like construction and forestry, and political investments to strengthen research and conservation efforts. Researchers say that it also requires making more people aware of the value of biodiversity for nature and for themselves.

Watershed conservation and restoration benefits everyone from the farmers whose fields are dissolving into the rivers to the species harmed by the sedimentation, according to Johnson.

“It makes everyone from the conservation people to the agriculture people happy,” he said.

Some partnerships are already in place. The state’s largest energy company, Alabama Power, “has a long track record of working with state and federal partners to protect listed species and their habitat,” Anthony Cook, Media Relations Principal wrote in an email. Alabama Power specifically refers to an endangered species, the Tulotoma Snail, whose recovery and downlisting “was largely due to water quality improvements made by Alabama Power”.

Political and financial investments are also needed to preserve and protect the endangered species. Researchers still don’t have a complete picture of the number of species and their distribution. There are simply too many to manage with the research funding available today, they warn.

Multiple attempts to get a comment from Republican and Democrat members of the Alabama State Senate Committee on Agriculture, Conservation and Forestry through phone calls and emails were unsuccessful.

Amid the continued decline in biodiversity, there is a broad consensus among scientists and environmentalists that Alabama is in a better place than it used to be. This is widely attributed to the passing of the Clean Water Act and Endangered Species Act in 1972 and 1973 – and the attention these laws brought to the issue of environmental protection and biodiversity.

“We're still losing components but in general, the habitats are probably in better shape than they were in the 60s and 70s,” said Paul Hartfield, a former Endangered Species Biologist at the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service.

But far more needs to be known about the freshwater animals living in the rivers and the threats still posed to them and their ecosystem, researchers contend.

“You can’t destroy some of these things or reduce them to almost minimal numbers left for survival and expect them to bounce back overnight”, Jim Williams, a former biologist at the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, said.

Biologists are just starting to see the improvements in water quality that the endangered species act and the clean water act caused. But at the same time, increased settlement and runoff from development are causing new threats to the freshwater ecosystems, Williams explains.

* * *

The danger facing the region’s vast biological diversity involves one of the most unique ecosystems in the United States, with a geological history dating back millions of years that sets it apart from others.

Between 65 million and 251 million years ago, during the Mesozoic Era, the southernmost part of the American Southeast was covered by ocean waters. The shoreline of the Atlantic Ocean fell where inland southern cities like Columbia, SC, Augusta, GA, and Tuscaloosa, AL, are today.

Then, starting around 115,000 years ago, during the last Ice Age, the northern part of the continental United States was covered by a thick layer of ice, known as the pleistocene glaciation, that wiped most out species, while the Southeast was left untouched.

These two things left a belt of land across the Southeast with exceptionally old and rich biological diversity that includes a larger roster of animals subject to endangerment.

While there are environmental issues throughout the country, the Southeast has 277 federally listed endangered and threatened species, according to data from the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Services. That is more than double any other region. The second tier is the Pacific region, including Hawaii – a place known for its rich but endangered wildlife.

The highest concentration of federally listed endangered species in the region is found in the rivers and creeks in Alabama and Tennessee. In some waterways, seven or eight endangered species live side by side. And those are only the federally listed. Many imperiled species are yet to be added to the US Fish and Wildlife Service's list.

The Southeastern United States also has an unusually high biodiversity – one third of the world's crayfish species and almost 40 percent of the world’s freshwater mussels are found in the region – and high rates of endemism, meaning that a species can only be found in very specific geographical locations. Many species are native to just one river or a couple of watersheds.

For some groups, like the region’s 275 different species of freshwater mussels and 348 species of snails, the level of endangerment is especially high. The data analysis found that almost one-third of the species of mussels and one fourth of the snails across the Southeast are ranked as imperiled or critically imperiled while 11.5 percent of the snails and 7.5 percent of the mussels are believed to already be extinct.

The loss of biodiversity reaches further than the endangerment and extinction of species of fishes and mussels. The loss of species is related to the quality of the water they live in and, by extension, the larger ecosystem.

“Biodiversity is kind of telling us what is wrong. And so the fact that we’re losing species can also mean the environment isn’t as suitable for us as it used to be,” said Katelyn Lawson from the Alabama Natural Heritage Program.

The animals both mirror the water quality and can help improve it, with different species feeding off and helping each other. Freshwater mussels, for example, act as nature’s vacuum cleaner. They feed on the microorganisms in the flowing water and in the process absorb potentially harmful pollutants – when their habitat is livable.

Freshwater mussels also depend on fish in the ecosystem for reproduction. The mussel larvae, scientifically known as glochidia, attach themselves to the gills of fish. There, they develop into juvenile mussels before detaching themselves into the river. Often specific species of mussels attach to specific species of host fish, so the disappearance of a fish can have serious consequences for the mussel. Conversely, fish are dependent on the mussels to filter and clean the water.

Some scientists focus on the idea that certain keystone species play a critical role in the structure and function of the ecosystem and that their disappearance would radically alter the overall system.

“When we start plucking them out willy-nilly through human caused extinctions, we are causing these crashes that we don’t even understand,” said Ben Raines, an environmental journalist from Alabama.

* * *

The factors behind the imperiled biodiversity are varied. Scientists point to the extensive damming of the region’s riverways as one of the main drivers behind the biodiversity crisis in the Southeast.

Since the beginning of the 20th century, more than 14,400 dams have been constructed on southeastern rivers and streams, according to a database maintained by the National Inventory of Dams. The construction of the dams peaked between 1950 and 1980.

The majority of the dams are small, privately owned constructions. But in between are large both private, federal and state-owned dams – 804 of them more than 50 feet tall. On average, the reservoirs they create can hold as much as 38,271,481,413 gallons of water that once coursed freely through waterways.

Dams, and especially large dams, radically alter the physical and chemical nature of aquatic habitats and streams, according to Michael Gangloff. What used to be free flowing rivers are transformed into a series of reservoirs with much lower levels of dissolved oxygen.

Dams “change things to the point where a lot of species that are dependent on flow for their life history or for providing sufficient levels of dissolved oxygen are no longer able to survive in their systems,” Gangloff said.

On the Coosa River alone, a series of seven large hydroelectric dams is believed to have caused the largest modern extinction event in North America, with 36 species disappearing after the damming of the river.

Historically, the Coosa River was, with almost 300 different aquatic species, one of the most biologically diverse rivers in the world. Today, it is the most developed in the state, with only a few free-flowing stretches remaining. In 2013, a WWF report pointed out the Coosa River as an example of dam constructions that neglect biodiversity.

Dams have long been considered a source of green energy but recent research shows that large, hydropower dams have a far greater climate impact than once thought, as reported by Inside Climate News.

“We've long been told by the industry and just kind of absorbed this message that hydropower is carbon free, there are no emissions,” Jack West, Policy and Advocacy Director at Alabama Rivers Alliance, a statewide network of groups working to protect and restore all of Alabama’s water resources. “That is not true, hydropower and reservoirs in particular can be large creators of methane in particular”.

On top of this, expanding urbanization, agriculture and an extensive forestry industry in the state have increased sedimentation in the rivers. Whereas in untouched nature, the trees and plants along the river would soak up much of the runoff water and the root systems catch the sedimentation.

Sedimentation and runoff can cloud the water while runoff from cities and agriculture causes increased levels of pollutants in the rivers. Both harm the species living there.

More recently, the restoration of the natural habitats of the species have been connected to climate change mitigation. A combination of more frequent heavy rainfalls and increased urbanization has created problems with stormwater runoff.

“Before we had all these streets and bridges and highways and rooftops, this area was very forested and an act was like a sponge,” said Ben Wegleitner from the Cahaba River Society. “As we've grown and spread out we've created a very hard surface where all that water is no longer soaking back into the ground.” According to Paul Johnson, the restorations of the watersheds flowing at the end of peoples’ backyards can also play an important role in absorbing the more extreme rainfall. “They could be the lifeline that saves your community,” he said.

* * *

While scientists can point to specific reasons for biodiversity loss, they remain unsure about the scale of the problem or how to fix it. This is partly because of political priorities, which further muddies the environmental waters. With almost incomparable numbers of species but little money for research and protection, there is room for improvement, according to Paul Johnson.

“We have all of the species and none of the money,” he said. “We’re the worst funded region nationally. We have more animal species listed than any other state”.

Researchers say that the significance of that can hardly be overstated.

“To slow the severe decline in globally significant southeastern freshwater ecosystems, more investment must be made rapidly in the region, both in funding and in conservation capacity,” concludes a 2019 study published in Global Ecology and Conservation.

According to a report by the Environmental Council of the States (ECOS) Alabama had the second lowest state environmental agency budget per capita in 2019. The state spent around $58 million on its state agency the Alabama Department of Environmental Management. This translates into $11.83 per citizen in Alabama, a smaller amount than in other states with high and endangered biodiversity, including California, Texas and Nevada. Only Georgia spent less. The other Southern states are spending at least $20 per capita with Mississippi topping the list with $103 per capita.

Since 1992, Alabama’s only state fund dedicated to land conservation, the Forever Wild Land Trust Program, has acquired land in Alabama for public use. But the Forever Wild program’s funding is capped at $15 million in any given year and Alabama has a smaller share of public conservation land – around 3.66 percent – than nearly all other Southeastern States.

Federal and state spending on federally listed aquatic species found within the southeast alone was lower than on species outside the region, the 2019 study found. Mussel species outside the region received 2.1 times more funding while non-southeastern fishes received 46.6 times more funding per species.

The state needs to make bigger investments if it wants to protect its endangered biodiversity, according to Mitchell Reid from the Nature Conservancy in Alabama. “Land is finite,” he said. “So, the more that we develop in the land, the more we protect, the more expensive it becomes to do the next project and to do larger and larger investments in protection.”

The limited funding stems from many competing interests in Alabama, according to environmentalists and other activists.

“Business and industry being major components of that and also transportation and tourism,” Ben Wegleitner, Cahaba River Society said. He said the different industries should work closer together to protect biodiversity – something they could all benefit from.

“There certainly is some competition for dollars and for the attention of decision makers,” Wegleitner adds.

Jack West from the Alabama Rivers Alliance echoes this.

“Our message does find traction,” he said. “However, it is an uphill battle, because there are vested interests and industries that don't want to see strong protections of our rivers that have for many years used our waters as sewers to dispose of industrial waste,” he said.

Neither chairperson of the Alabama Senate Committee Agriculture, Conservation and Forestry David Sessions (R), Vice Chairperson Jack W. Williams (R), Ranking Minority Member Bobby D. Singleton (D) or the office of the Alabama Senate Democratic Caucus replied to requests for comment.

But some of the pushback from environmental groups have met success in recent years.

In 2013, the Alabama Rivers Alliance and American took Alabama Power and the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) to court. The environmental groups claimed that Alabama Power’s recently renewed dam license on the Coosa River didn’t improve the conditions on the river by securing adequate oxygen levels and enough flow.

Five years later, the U.S. Court of Appeal in Washington DC vacated Alabama Power’s dam license. It was determined that FERC hadn’t considered the previous impact the dams had had on the rivers and had ignored evidence that relicensing could lead to the extinction of multiple endangered mussels and cause perilously low dissolved oxygen levels. They had renewed the dam license in 2013.

The court ruled that FERC had to redo the required analysis of the environmental impact of the dams, called a NEPA analysis, and biological opinion before the license can be renewed.

Currently, FERC has released a draft for the new NEPA analysis and organizations like Alabama Rivers Alliance have submitted their comments. The next step is for FERC to complete the final version – which will be the basis for the new license. But if Alabama Rivers Alliance doesn't see enough improvements, they will be ready to go back to court.

“The saga about the licensing of those dams is still playing out and we hope to see a license that is fully protective of a of the river and the species that depend on it,” Jack West said. “And if we don't, we may have to go through it all over again.”

Alabama Power has been putting some iterations into the dams to increase water quality around their dams. In 2017, they installed a new aeration system in the Logan Martin Lake to improve the levels of dissolved oxygen in the water, Alabama News Center reported.

But the changes are not to a level that leaves aquatic life unharmed, said Sarah Stokes, one of the lawyers from the Southern Environmental Law Center representing the petitioners American Rivers and Alabama Rivers Alliance.

“They are papering over their mistakes instead of fixing the substantive mistakes of their actions,” she said.

Alabama Power rejects this. The energy company responsibly generates electricity through our sustainable operations and compliance obligations, Anthony Cook, Alabama Power wrote in an email.

“Alabama is one of the most ecologically diverse places in the U.S. and we are committed to preserving the beauty and richness of our state’s natural landscape. As one of Alabama’s largest managers of land and water resources, we work to strengthen wildlife habitat and protect river ecosystems,” he continued.

As the federal legislation passed in the 1970’s has helped slow down the deterioration, there is a space for improvements, explains Michell Reid, State Director of the Nature Conservancy in Alabama. “We have the capability of understanding what is in our river systems, and we have the mechanisms to make sure that we're sort of collectively doing a better job of ensuring that those systems are protected,” he said.

* * *

The progress and efforts to restore and reverse decades of damage to biodiversity becomes clear in a small building in Perry Country, AL. It is the home of the Alabama Aquatic Biodiversity Center, a research center that has spent the past 15 years working with some of the state’s most endangered aquatic species.

They have cultured more than 190,000 mussels from 12 federally listed species and 145,000 snails from six listed species and released them, not just in Alabama but also Tennessee, Mississippi and Louisiana.

The animals are raised from larvae to mussel in a series of plastic buckets and rubber tubes that secure the sufficient flow of oxygen in the water. All tailored to specific stages in the life of a juvenile mussel.

“A lot of refurbished plastic,” one of the center’s employees, Michael Buntin, calls it.

The setups have to be simple designs. Because of the center's rural location, the employees need to be able to fix it themselves. And when it comes to raising rare aquatic species in a laboratory there is no golden industry standard. The researchers come up with solutions along the way – figuring out what works best for each individual species.

Johnson and Barker-Jones recent dive into the Coosa River, was a part of those efforts. They measured and photographed the animals before putting them back into the river. The two scientists were tracking the mussels’ growth and thus the success of the reintroduction.

The six mussels they found that day had on average almost doubled their biomass in a year – a very satisfying result. But the small victory is only the beginning of a yearlong process to establish a stable and reproducing population in a single location.

“You have to have a sustainable population and it has to be sustainable for a decade. Then the service can begin the process to do the downlist,” Johnson explained. This usually takes decades.

Johnson knows this better than most. In his decade-long career as a conservation biologist, he has downlisted only two species from the most critical category, endangered, to threatened on the US Fish and Wildlife Services list of endangered species.

But the center has managed to establish self-supporting and reproducing populations of several species in multiple rivers. And Paul Johnson hopes that he can soon downlist another snail.

According to Paul Hartfield, it is time to prioritize the environment and protect what is left.

“In most cases it's not really a restoration. It's deciding what we want it to be? How much function can we get back to it?” Hartfield said.
